# Anatomical Terminology

## Learning Objectives

- Define anatomical position and use it as the reference posture.
- Apply directional terms accurately in first-year anatomy descriptions.
- Distinguish planes, sections, laterality terms, and movement language.

## Key Concepts

- Anatomical position: upright, eyes forward, arms at sides, palms anterior, feet forward.
- Directional pairs: superior/inferior, anterior/posterior, medial/lateral, proximal/distal, superficial/deep.
- Laterality terms: ipsilateral, contralateral, unilateral, bilateral.
- Planes: sagittal, midsagittal, coronal, transverse, and oblique.

## High-Yield Facts

- The thumb is lateral in anatomical position.
- Proximal and distal are most useful for limbs.
- Midsagittal divides the body into equal left and right halves.
- Transverse sections are commonly called axial sections in imaging.

## Common Mistakes

- Describing the palms as posterior in anatomical position.
- Confusing medial/lateral with proximal/distal.
- Saying flexion increases joint angle.
- Using left and right without specifying the patient perspective.

## Application Examples

- The sternum is anterior to the vertebral column.
- The heart is medial to the lungs.
- The wrist is distal to the elbow.
- The skin is superficial to skeletal muscle.

## Oral Exam Style Prompts

- Describe anatomical position.
- Explain the difference between sagittal, coronal, and transverse planes.
- Compare the patella and popliteal fossa using directional terms.
- Give an example of ipsilateral and contralateral structures.
